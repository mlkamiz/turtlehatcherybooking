Info on Sea Turtles

Sea Turtles scientifically named as Chelonioidea are a group of reptiles adapted to living in the marine environment. Sea turtles have streamlined bodies that help them swim efficiently in water. They have protective shells and can migrate long distances. Their limbs are flippers, which they use for swimming and steering. Sea turtles have a special way of breathing that lets them stay underwater for a long time. They mate in the sea and lay eggs on beaches. These amazing creatures can be found in all oceans except the polar regions.

Natural Benefits

Sea turtles are vital for healthy oceans. They are known as "keystone species" because they play a crucial role in their environment and affect other species. Removing a keystone species can disturb the natural balance and impact other wildlife and plants in various ways. Sea turtles provide a habitat for various organisms like barnacles, small crustaceans, remoras, algae, and diatoms. Their long migrations help transport these species. Additionally, sea turtles act as a shelter for fish, protecting them from predators.

Artificial Benefits

Sea turtles are vital to coastal communities worldwide, with cultural significance and revered as ancestors by indigenous cultures. They also boost the income of coastal residents through turtle-watching ecotourism. Apart from their ecological role, sea turtles are adored for their charm. Watching them nest, swim, or witness hatchlings reaching the sea is enchanting and unforgettable. Sea turtles are essential for our planet, completing our oceans.

Turtles in Sri Lanka
1.

Out of the seven living sea turtle species recorded in the world, Sri Lanka is blessed to have five species reported along the beautiful coastal belt of the island. Namely, the Hawksbill Turtle, the Loggerhead Turtle, the Leatherback Trutle, the Green Turtle, and the Olive Ridley Turtle

2.

Of these the Green Turtle is the most common while the Loggerhead Turtle is the rarest

3.

Depending on the season, Rekawa, Hikkaduwa, Mirissa, Kosgoda, Beruwala and Trincomalee can be considered as the best places in the island to catch a glimpse of these great turtles

4.

The main breeding season is from September to mid-April on the West coast of the island and from February to June on the East coast.

5.

As far as the Department of Wildlife of Sri Lanka is concerned the Turtle Conservation Project (TCP) is the sole organisation that is authorised to run turtle hatcheries in Sri Lanka. However there are a number of private individuals who have taken up this activity as a business venture.
